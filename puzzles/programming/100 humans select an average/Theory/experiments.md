**Yes, this is very well-documented in behavioral game theory.**

The game is the **p-beauty contest** (or Keynesian beauty contest guessing game), most famously studied experimentally by **Rosemarie Nagel** (1995) and later by **Colin Camerer, Teck-Hua Ho**, and many others.

### Key Experimental Findings (Semi-Formal Summary)

In the standard setup (choose a number 0–100, win by being closest to **p × average**, where p is usually 2/3 or 1/2):

- **First-round averages with inexperienced participants** typically fall in the **22–40** range, most commonly **25–35**.

- Nagel’s original 1995 experiment (p=2/3) found an average around **35**.
- Many replications with students and general populations show means around **30–36** in the first round.
- Even with smart groups (chess players, economists, etc.), first-round averages rarely drop below ~20–25.

- The distribution is **not uniform** — there are clear spikes at “obvious” level-k numbers: ~50 (level-0/random), ~33 (level-1), ~22 (level-2), ~15 (level-3), and sometimes 0/1 (infinite reasoning).

**Level-k / Cognitive Hierarchy models** fit the data much better than perfect rationality. Most people stop at level 1–2 reasoning. Very few go beyond level 3–4 in one-shot or low-time-pressure settings.

### Factors That Influence Results

- **Time pressure / deliberation time**: Shorter time → higher averages (more level-0 and level-1 thinking). More time or repeated play → lower numbers.
- **Demographics / Population**:
  - Students vs. general public: Similar, but experts (e.g., chess grandmasters) are only slightly better.
  - Children: Start a bit higher but show comparable learning trajectories.
- **Group size**: Larger groups sometimes lead to more rational (lower) choices.
- **p-value** (the multiplier): Lower p (e.g. 1/2 instead of 2/3) leads to lower averages.
- **Repetition with feedback**: Averages converge toward equilibrium over rounds, but slowly and rarely reach 0 in one-shot or newspaper experiments.

### On "Unexpected" or Interesting Findings

I didn't find anything specifically about **fragrance in the room** or direct "men think about sex more" effects in the core beauty contest literature (those sound more like priming studies from social psychology). However, researchers have documented:

- **Gender effects**: Mixed, sometimes small differences in reasoning depth.
- **Cognitive ability / IQ**: Correlates with deeper reasoning, but not overwhelmingly.
- **Professional background**: Economists, game theorists, and chess players do better on average but still show substantial bounded rationality in the first round.

The main robust takeaway across hundreds of experiments is exactly what you observed informally: **Real humans show a mixture of reasoning levels**, and sophisticated players succeed by correctly estimating the **distribution of sophistication** in the population (a Bayesian/meta-reasoning task), not by assuming everyone is perfectly rational.

This is why the pure Nash equilibrium (0) is almost never observed in one-shot play with real people — it's a terrible prediction unless everyone is a game theorist who assumes everyone else is too.



In the beauty contest / guessing game experiments, "doing better" is defined pragmatically as:

Having your number closer to the actual target (p × average of all submitted numbers).
Winning the prize (or sharing it) more often.
Having a smaller absolute error from the realized average.

It is not about how elegant or deeply rational your reasoning is in theory. It's about prediction accuracy of real human behavior.

This supports your observation: the best performers are not necessarily the ones with the highest raw intelligence or deepest iterative reasoning. They are the ones who best estimate the actual Gaussian distribution of player types (how many Level-0, Level-1, Level-2 thinkers are in the room).
Assuming "everyone else is like me" (a common bias among very smart people) is often a losing strategy here.
This is why the game is so revealing: it rewards accurate folk psychology more than pure deductive power. A sociopath or skilled manipulator who understands crowd stupidity and inattention might actually have an edge in the one-shot version.
