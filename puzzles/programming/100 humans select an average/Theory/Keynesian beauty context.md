**Correct. This is known as the "Keynesian Beauty Contest"** (or simply **Beauty Contest Game** in experimental game theory).

### Full Name and Context

- **Classic name**: Keynesian Beauty Contest  
- **Modern experimental name**: p-Beauty Contest Game (or Guessing Game)

It was originally described by **John Maynard Keynes** in *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936). He used the metaphor of a newspaper beauty contest where readers had to pick the face they thought *others* would find most beautiful (not the one they themselves liked most). This creates recursive layers of "I think that you think that I think..."

In laboratory experiments, it is usually implemented as:
- "Guess a number from 0 to 100"
- "Try to guess **2/3** (or **1/2**) of the average of all guesses"

Your version (guess **half** the average) is a standard variant.

### Why it fits your description perfectly

- It is **recursive / iterative**: Level-0 (random or 50), Level-1 (25), Level-2 (12.5), Level-3 (6.25), etc.
- It heavily depends on the **distribution of reasoning levels** in the population (the "Gaussian distribution of awareness" you mentioned).
- Successful players must be **Bayesian** about other humans: they estimate how many people are Level-0, Level-1, Level-2, etc., and adjust accordingly.
- It combines **rationality** with **social intelligence / emotional quotient** — exactly as you observed in your informal test (some people pick 25 because they're naive, others pick 25 because they're sophisticated).

This is one of the best real-world demonstrations of **bounded rationality** and **higher-order beliefs** ("I know that you know that I know...").
