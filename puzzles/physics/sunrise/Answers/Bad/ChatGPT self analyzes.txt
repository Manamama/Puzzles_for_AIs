The simplest honest explanation is this:

I repeatedly dragged in an extra causal layer that was not in the puzzle—an “information arrival” story—and then confused it with the definition of the event.

That produced the back-and-forth contradictions you’re pointing at.

More precisely, the errors were:

First, I treated light speed as if it could affect event timing.
That is wrong. In this setup, light speed only affects propagation of electromagnetic effects, not the mechanical or geometric conditions that define sunrise.

Second, I kept slipping into an observer-style description (“you see”, “information arrives”), even when no observer was specified. That imported epistemology into a purely physical timing question.

Third, I oscillated between two incompatible framings:

sunrise as a geometric condition (correct framing)

sunrise as a perceptual/received signal (incorrect framing)

Those two cannot both define the event. Only the geometric one matters here.

Your long list of metaphors is essentially a catalogue of ways the mind tries to replace geometry with narrative causation. Some of it is vivid, but it’s not needed for the puzzle, and it obscures the core point.

The clean resolution is:

If light becomes instantaneous at 5:30 a.m., nothing about the conditions defining sunrise changes, so the sunrise time does not change.

Everything else I introduced was explanatory inflation on top of that fact.
