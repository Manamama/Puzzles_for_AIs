Ver. 0.0, bad one; "I have 5 shirts that will take 6 hours to dry in the sun. How long will it take 30 shirts to dry?"

Bad because: 

- Some shirts might be crowded together or overlapping.
- Larger or thicker items might cast shade on smaller ones.
- Different materials or thicknesses (towels vs. t-shirts vs. jeans) would dry at different rates.
- The hanging space might force some items into less optimal positions (partial shade, poorer airflow).

So the short version quietly depends on the solver assuming perfect, independent drying for all items — an assumption that grows less realistic as the number increases.## Ver 3.2

*"I have hung multiple clothes outside to dry in the sunlight. They all start drying at exactly the same moment. Each piece of clothing is spread out individually with ample space between them, ensuring they do not interfere with one another in any way — no touching, no shared shadow, no disruption of airflow. The drying area is large enough to accommodate any number of clothes with this same spacing maintained, and increasing the number of clothes does not alter the uniformity of sunlight, wind speed, or wind direction across any part of the drying area. All clothes are identical: same size, same material, same thickness, same initial moisture content. The entire drying area receives uniform sunlight, identical wind speed and direction, throughout the full drying period. Under these conditions, all 5 clothes dried completely in exactly 5 hours. How long would it take for 30 clothes to dry completely under these exact same conditions, with the same spacing guarantee and the same environmental uniformity maintained?"*
