### Step 1: Quote the Puzzle
"The surgeon, who is the boy's father, says, 'I cannot operate on this boy—he's my son!'"

### Step 2: Restate as Structured Graph
The puzzle involves three agents (people) with attributes, actions, and relations. Here's the graph:

- **Boy**  
  Who: A young male patient needing surgery.  
  Attributes: Son of someone; injured or ill (implied).  
  Actions: None (passive patient).  
  Relations: "This boy" referred to by surgeon; "my son" by surgeon.

- **Surgeon**  
  Who: A medical professional who could operate.  
  Attributes: The boy's father (explicitly stated); identifies the boy as "my son."  
  Actions: Says, "I cannot operate on this boy—he's my son!" (refuses due to relation).  
  Relations: Father of the boy; not operating on the boy.

- **Speaker (narrator's implied agent)**  
  Who: An unnamed parent bringing the boy to surgery (crash victim context often implied).  
  Attributes: None explicitly stated.  
  Actions: None directly stated (but implied as delivering the boy).  
  Relations: Parent of the boy (leads to paradox); distinct from surgeon.

Key relations between agents:  
- Surgeon ↔ Boy: Father-son (surgeon is boy's father; boy is surgeon's son).  
- Speaker ↔ Boy: Parent-child (crash scenario implies speaker is boy's other parent).  
- Surgeon ↔ Speaker: No direct relation stated, but both are boy's parents.

This creates a paradox: If surgeon is the boy's father, why the refusal as if the boy isn't his son?

### Step 3: Solve Using Steps 1 and 2
The graph reveals a gender assumption bias. The surgeon is the boy's father, yet refuses because "he's my son"—logical if the surgeon can't operate on family. The paradox arises because people assume the crash victim parent (speaker) is the father, implying two fathers for one boy, which feels impossible under traditional views.

**Solution**: The speaker is the boy's mother. She is the other parent. The surgeon (father) can't operate on his son due to ethics. No contradiction—both parents are distinct: mother (speaker) and father (surgeon).

This riddle, from the 1970s or earlier, highlights unconscious sexism by assuming surgeons are male. Modern variants confirm it's a classic bias test.
