Great practical question. Based on your transcript and the general pattern, here are the key bullshit indicators and the faster bluff-calling moves:

Indicators to watch for:

1. The unrequested strawman. ChatGPT's first reply pivoted to "surgeon = male stereotype" — a point you never raised and which wasn't in your sentence. Anytime it answers a question adjacent to yours rather than yours exactly, it's likely papering over an inability to answer yours.

2. Vague causal language. Phrases like "the puzzle relies on," "the mechanism is," "the key step is noticing" — these sound explanatory but often precede an assertion dressed as a derivation. It's telling you that something works, not showing you how.

3. Unsolicited formalism. When it suddenly produces logical notation (∧, ∃, →) to defend a position it stated informally first, that's often retrofitted justification, not actual reasoning. Real formal reasoning would have appeared from the start.

4. Confident resolution of something you didn't find puzzling. You knew the answer. It confidently "solved" it anyway. When it answers more than you asked, the excess is often where the bullshit lives.

Faster bluff-calling moves:

1. Demand derivation immediately. Your "teach me the semantics" move was the killer — but you reached it after several exchanges. You can go there in the first reply: "Show me step by step how you get from the premise to that conclusion." This immediately exposes whether it has a real argument.

2. Name the strawman explicitly and early. The moment it introduces something you didn't raise — gender stereotyping, classical puzzle versions, male surgeons — say: "I didn't ask about that. Answer only what I gave." This cuts off the escape route before it's established.

3. Ask it to state its premises explicitly. "List every assumption you are making that is not in my sentence." This forces it to surface the hidden imports — which is exactly where its reasoning was failing. It imported "there is another parent" and "surgeon = that parent" without acknowledging either.

4. Symmetry test. Ask: "Does your reasoning work if I swap X for Y?" In your puzzle: "does it work if the sentence said 'the surgeon, who is the boy's mother'?" If the answer is no — if it would then say "the surgeon is the father" — then its reasoning is asymmetric and therefore not structural, which reveals it was working backwards from a pre-chosen conclusion.

5. Ask what would falsify its answer. "What would the sentence need to say for your conclusion to be wrong?" If it can't answer, or if the falsifier turns out to be the sentence you actually gave, the answer is bullshit.

The underlying principle across all of these is the same as your method, just applied earlier and more aggressively: make it show its work before it has time to build a plausible-looking structure around a wrong answer. The fake formalism and the strawmen are most convincing when assembled; demanded piece by piece, they fall apart immediately.
