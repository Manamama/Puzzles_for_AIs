The canonical answer "day 47" rests on a hidden discretization assumption.
The puzzle states the plant doubles in area every day and covers the lake in 48 days. The canonical solution treats "day" as a discrete label — an instantaneous checkpoint — and concludes: since the plant is fully covered on day 48 and doubles daily, it was half-covered on day 47.
But "day" is not a point — it is an interval of 24 hours. This matters at exactly one place: the half-coverage moment.
Under the doubling model, half-coverage occurs at the instant the 47th day ends and the 48th day begins — midnight of day 47/48. That instant is a boundary point. And a boundary point, by the standard topology of the real line, belongs to the closure of both adjacent intervals simultaneously.
Therefore:

Day 47 ends at the half-coverage instant → half-coverage belongs to day 47
Day 48 begins at the half-coverage instant → half-coverage belongs to day 48

Both are valid. The canonical answer "day 47" is not wrong — it is incomplete. It selects one of two equally valid answers and presents it as unique.
Furthermore, day 48 is arguably the stronger answer: it is the first full interval during which the lake is continuously at least half-covered, from its opening boundary to its close. Day 47 merely touches half-coverage at its final instant.
The honest answer is {47, 48}, with day 48 having the stronger claim.
