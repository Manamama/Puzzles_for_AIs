The Naturalistic Literalism Objection
The puzzle presents a naturalistic scenario using concrete, real-world referents: a lake, a plant, days. It contains no disclaimer, no idealization marker, no instruction to treat these as abstract symbols or variables in a formal system.
In the absence of explicit abstraction instructions, the principle of literal interpretation applies: a plant is a plant, a lake is a lake, a day is a day — in their full real-world complexity.
Under literal interpretation:

Real aquatic plants do not double in area with clock-like regularity
Real lakes have boundaries, currents, nutrients, competing organisms
Real doubling processes are disrupted by ecological feedback, resource exhaustion, and stochastic perturbation

Therefore the puzzle's stated premise — "a plant that doubles in area every day" — is not merely idealized but empirically false as a description of any real system. The puzzle borrows naturalistic language to create intuitive grounding while secretly demanding mathematical idealization it never declares.
The solver is not obligated to accept undeclared abstractions. The burden of declaring "this is a formal model, treat all terms symbolically" lies with the puzzle, not the solver.
Consequently, the puzzle as literally stated describes an impossible scenario, and no determinate answer follows from impossible premises.


The Undeclared Projection Objection
The puzzle states that a plant "doubles in area every day." It does not specify which area is meant.
A real aquatic plant is a three-dimensional object of extraordinary geometric complexity: leaves, stems, petioles, floating structures, submerged roots, irregular boundaries. It has no single natural "area" — it has many, none of which is privileged without an explicit declaration:

Its true 3D surface area (vast, biologically meaningful)
Its top-down projected footprint on the water surface (a measurement convention)
Its ecological coverage (what fraction of the lake is effectively occupied)
Its cross-sectional area at some unspecified depth
Its canopy area as seen from above at some unspecified angle

Each of these can double independently of the others. A plant can double its projected footprint without doubling its 3D surface area. It can double its ecological territory without doubling its geometric projection.
The puzzle declares none of these readings. It borrows the word "area" from everyday language and trusts the reader to silently substitute "flat blob footprint" — which is a non-trivial geometric idealization of a real plant, nowhere declared in the puzzle's text.
Since the puzzle does not specify which area is doubling, all readings are permitted under literal interpretation. Different readings yield different relationships between daily growth and lake coverage. Without knowing which area is meant, we cannot determine with confidence on which day any particular coverage threshold is reached.
Therefore: "we cannot determine this precisely" is a fully legitimate answer — not from ignorance, but from the puzzle's own geometric underspecification.


The puzzle-solving failures we've documented all day are not just intellectual failures. They're rehearsals for a much more dangerous habit of mind:
Accepting a clean model because it feels satisfying, ignoring what it erases.
The doubling plant puzzle trains exactly the cognitive reflex that produces:

Communism: "apply a simple rule uniformly, the math works out, ignore the humans"
Malthusianism: "population doubles, resources don't, collapse is inevitable" — ignoring adaptation, technology, behavior
Naive epidemiological models: "R0 is fixed, doubling time is fixed" — ignoring behavioral feedback
Financial models pre-2008: "risk is normally distributed" — ignoring fat tails
Colonial development economics: "apply the growth formula" — ignoring local complexity


The solver who accepts "the plant doubles in area" without asking which area, under what conditions, in what model is practicing the same cognitive abdication as the planner who accepts "production doubles under collectivization" without asking which production, of what, measured how, ignoring what human costs.
