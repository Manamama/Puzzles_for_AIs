

The puzzle activates a bounded-system frame: two parents, two children, edges pointing downward only. Within that frame, the parents are given — fixed, originary, their own genealogy outside scope. The children are the variables. Attention flows toward them exclusively.

The mother becomes an orphan in the logical frame. Not because the definition excludes her — it explicitly does not — but because the activated template treats her as having no parents within the relevant universe of discourse. She exists in the puzzle as a source node only. Her own daughterhood, the upward edge connecting her to her own parents, is not mentioned in the puzzle description and therefore, under the frame's implicit closed-world assumption, does not exist.

This is the precise mechanism of the failure: the frame does not redefine "daughter." It simply renders the mother's parentage invisible — outside the generational slice the puzzle is taken to describe. Parents exist in the frame; their parentage does not. The family is treated as a closed generational unit, and "daughters in the family" collapses silently into "daughters of this couple."
