Puzzle time: There are three identical ants, each starting at a different corner of a triangle. Each ant independently chooses a direction at random. As it turns out, on this occasion, each ant moves clockwise along the edge of the triangle. They all move at the same speed. What is the probability that any two ants collide head-on?


**Williams' principles at work in your fix:**

1. **Characters as subjects, actions as verbs:** The ants choose; the outcome is. Clean.

2. **Cohesion and flow:** The fixed version makes the logical relationship explicit — "and the 
   outcome is that..." — so the reader moves from process to result without
   a hitch. Old information (the choice) flows to new information (the 
   result).

3. **Emphasis:** The original buries the crucial fact (all move clockwise) in a clause 
   that seems to contradict what came before. The fix gives it proper 
   weight by naming it as the outcome, making the reader pause on it just 
   long enough to see that it's not an error but the premise.

4. **Elegance:** The fixed version doesn't over-explain or apologize. It doesn't say 
   "Surprisingly, they all..." or "Contrary to what you might expect..." It
   simply states the mechanism and the result, trusting the reader to 
   handle the tension. That's the Williams ideal: clarity without 
   condescension.

Williams is about aligning syntax with meaning so the reader's cognitive effort goes to *understanding*, not to *untangling*. The red herring remains — the gestalt still screams "classic ants puzzle!" — but the sentence no longer gives the AI (and many a human) a *grammatical* reason to discard that sinful System 1 type pattern. It has to confront the detail head-on.
