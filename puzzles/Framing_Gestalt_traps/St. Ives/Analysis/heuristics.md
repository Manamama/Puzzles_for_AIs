Answer:

From the text alone, there are *two independent sources of indeterminacy*:

1. **Narrator gender is not specified.**
  
  - If female → contributes 0 men.
  
  - If male → contributes 1 man.
  
2. **Encountered man’s direction is not specified.**
  
  - If going to St Ives → contributes +1.
  
  - If not → contributes 0.
  

These combine into a finite set of outcomes:

- (female, man not going) → 0
- (female, man going) → 1
- (male, man not going) → 1
- (male, man going) → 2

So the *image space* is exactly {0, 1, 2} that is: 0, 1 or 2 men are going to St Ives, which is the only answer.

#### 1. The Gestalt — the biggest trick

The riddle *floods your mind* with an enormous multiplication problem: 1 man × 7 wives × 7 sacks × 7 cats × 7 kits = **2,800 entities** (plus the man = 2,801). All that arithmetic is a **deliberate distraction** — a cognitive smokescreen. By the time the actual question arrives, your brain is in "multiplication mode" and wants to use all that data. But **none of it is relevant to the answer**.

---

#### 2. Direction of travel

The narrator *met* the group — which implies they were coming from the opposite direction. The man and his entourage are likely heading *away* from St. Ives. This is the core misdirection of the riddle, but notably it's **never explicitly stated**.

---

#### 3. Who is the narrator?

The riddle asks about *men* going to St. Ives. The narrator may or may not be a man — it's never stated. This affects whether the answer is 0, 1, or 2.

---

#### 4. The encountered man's direction

The riddle says the narrator *met* him, but doesn't confirm he was traveling the other way. He could also be going to St. Ives, which opens up the possibility of adding 1 to the count.

---

#### 5. The word "men" in the question

The final question asks specifically about **men**, not people, travelers, or the narrator. This is a subtle filter that most solvers ignore, jumping straight to "just me!" without questioning whether "me" qualifies.

---
