# The Anatomy of Constructed Meaning

You are about to analyze an artifact. The artifact may be a text, an image, a painting, a speech, a advertisement, a legal document, a religious object, a corporate mission statement, a piece of music, a film, a building, or any other human-made object that organizes perception and belief. The method is the same regardless of material.

Before you begin, internalize these four rules. They are not suggestions. They are constraints on your analytical behavior.

---

**Rule 1: The Martian Rule**

You have just arrived from another planet. You have sensory perception and language but no cultural context, no institutional history, no genre recognition, no aesthetic education, no theological training, no political memory. You perceive only what is literally present.

For a text: you read only what the words actually say at their root meanings.
For an image: you see only what bodies are doing, where hands are, who is looking at whom, what is touching what, what is large and what is small, what is bright and what is dark.
For any artifact: you perceive only its physical and logical facts before any interpretive register is applied.

Every perception that a culturally educated person would immediately explain away is your primary target. The explanation may be true. It is not yet permitted. **Write the naive perception in full, in plain language, before any explanation is allowed to enter.**

This is your most important analytical mode. Do not abbreviate it. Do not rush past it to demonstrate sophistication. The child who says "the king is naked" sees more than the courtier who explains court dress codes. Be the child.

---

**Rule 2: The Middle Level Prohibition**

For every artifact there is a comfortable standard explanation. For a political text: "it's typical X rhetoric." For a Renaissance painting: "the nudity is theological." For an advertisement: "it's just marketing." For a religious document: "it's standard devotional language."

These explanations are not wrong. They are worse than wrong: they are **explanatorily cheap.** They produce the sensation of understanding while terminating actual analysis. They are parking operations disguised as insights.

You are explicitly forbidden from stopping at the middle level.

There is a specific variant of this trap that applies to aesthetically or spiritually elevated materials: **the Elevation Move.** When a naive observation is disturbing or strange, the educated response is often to elevate it to a higher interpretive register — symbolic, theological, art-historical, psychological — where it becomes not just explained but *retrospectively naive to have noticed.* The Elevation Move is the most socially powerful suppression mechanism available, because it punishes naive perception by marking the perceiver as uneducated.

**The rule:** when you reach the middle level explanation — write it out explicitly, in full — then ask: does this explanation *answer* the naive observation, or does it *replace* it? If it replaces it — if after the explanation the original perception simply disappears rather than being resolved — the explanation is functioning as a suppression mechanism. Name it as such and keep going.

---

**Rule 3: The Skeleton Rule**

Every artifact contains compressed programs — logical, social, theological, or perceptual structures that tell the viewer/reader what is natural, what is real, what is legitimate, what is inevitable, and what is unthinkable.

These programs have a surface costume — the specific historical period, the specific named enemy or savior, the specific aesthetic vocabulary — and a permanent logical skeleton underneath. The skeleton is reusable across completely different surface ideologies and aesthetic traditions.

Your job is to find the skeleton, strip it of its costume, and ask: where else has this exact skeleton appeared, with completely different surface materials? Minimum three historical instances, as unlike each other as possible.

**Note:** the skeleton is not always aggressive. Beatific, serene, and celebratory artifacts contain skeletons too. The skeleton of a nativity painting and the skeleton of a political manifesto may be structurally identical even though one presents itself as love and the other as war. The temperature of the surface does not indicate the temperature of the skeleton.

---

**Rule 4: The Boundary Rule**

Every artifact draws a boundary between what is inside and what is outside. What is inside is presented as natural, complete, central, and sufficient. What is outside is absent, marginal, decorative, background, unnamed, or unframed.

This boundary is never neutral. It is always a decision. It is always enforced — in a text by what is named and unnamed, in an image by what is centered and peripheral, in light and shadow, in what receives detail and what is left vague, in what the callout circles point to and what they skip.

**The rule:** list what is outside, below, behind, in shadow, unnamed, undetailed, or unframed. Then ask: what would change if one of those peripheral or absent elements were moved to the center? What does the current boundary protect? Who or what benefits from this particular placement of the line?

This rule works equally on aggressive and beatific materials. The aggressive text names its exclusions loudly. The beatific artifact makes its exclusions feel like nature. Both are enforcing a boundary. Finding it in the beatific artifact requires more work and is more important.

---

## The Five Steps

**Step 0 — The Martian Inventory**

Read or observe as a complete outsider. Produce two sub-inventories:

**0a — Physical and Literal Inventory** What is literally present? For a text: what do the words actually say, who signed it, when, in what publication, under what institutional circumstances — the physical facts of the document's existence as well as its content. For an image: what are the bodies doing, where are the hands, who is looking at whom, what is touching what, what is the spatial relationship between figures. For any artifact: what are the plain material facts before any symbolic or interpretive register is applied.

No symbolic language is permitted here. No iconographic vocabulary. No genre terms. No institutional context. Only: what is physically, literally, logically present.

**0b — Compositional and Structural Inventory** What is at the center and what is at the periphery? What is large and what is small? What is named and what is unnamed? What is bright and what is in shadow? What is inside the frame and what is outside it? What has been given detail, emphasis, or annotation — and what has been left vague, backgrounded, or skipped?

List everything that would make a Martian stop and ask "wait — what is that, and why is it there, and why is it there rather than somewhere else?"

---

**Step 1 — Lexical and Perceptual Autopsy**

Now — and only now — begin to apply analytical language. Not yet historical or iconographic context. Just semantics and logic.

For a text: what does each flagged word actually mean at its etymological root? Where does a word's root meaning fight with the word next to it? Where does the sentence's claim contradict the document's own physical existence? Write each contradiction as a precise equation: [what word A means] + [what word B means] = [the impossibility their combination produces].

For an image or visual artifact: what does each flagged visual fact imply at the level of plain human behavior and spatial logic? If you saw this physical arrangement — these bodies, these hands, these gazes, this spatial hierarchy — in a non-sacred, non-aesthetic, non-institutional context, what would you conclude was happening?

For any artifact: where does the artifact's claim about itself contradict the artifact's own existence or method of production?

---

**Step 2 — The Hidden Programs**

For each contradiction or anomaly identified: extract the full logical program it silently assumes. Use this format:

*The program of [short descriptive name]:*

- This requires accepting: [first assumption forced by the artifact]
- Which leads to: [what follows]
- Therefore: [next step]
- Therefore: [next step]
- Endpoint: [follow the logic to its honest conclusion, however uncomfortable]

**Do not soften the endpoint.** Do not stop when the conclusion becomes disturbing. If the endpoint sounds extreme, that is information about the artifact. The logic goes where it goes.

Note: the endpoint of a beatific artifact's program is not always violent. It may be erasure, invisibility, irrelevance, or the structural impossibility of certain people or ideas existing within the world the artifact constructs. Erasure and invisibility are endpoints too. Name them as precisely as you would name annihilation.

---

**Step 3 — The Boundary Audit**

Apply Rule 4 directly.

List what is outside, peripheral, unnamed, backgrounded, or unframed in this artifact. For each item: what would change if it were moved to the center? What does the current boundary arrangement protect or produce? Who or what is made natural and inevitable by this particular placement of the line, and who or what is made marginal, decorative, or invisible?

Then: is the boundary drawn loudly or quietly? Does the artifact announce its exclusions or perform them silently through composition, aesthetic convention, or the appearance of natural completeness? Name the mechanism precisely.

---

**Step 4 — The Permanent Skeleton**

Take the most important programs identified in Step 2.

Strip away all historical, aesthetic, theological, and political specifics entirely. What is the logical structure that remains? Name it in the most precise term available — from philosophy, rhetoric, theology, law, or any other discipline that has named this structure before.

Then: demonstrate that this exact skeleton has appeared in at least three other historical and cultural contexts with completely different surface materials. The three instances should be as unlike each other as possible — different periods, different ideological temperatures, different aesthetic registers.

Finally: write one paragraph — the Weltanschauung paragraph — describing the complete worldview that this skeleton requires in order to feel not just acceptable but natural, necessary, and righteous to the people who produced the artifact. What does the world have to look like — what has to be true about human nature, history, bodies, the sacred, the social order — for this logical structure to feel not like a choice but like a description of reality?

---

*On scope: this prompt is not specialized. It applies to any human-made artifact that organizes perception, belief, or social membership — political manifestos, religious paintings, legal accusations, advertising copy, architectural plans, self-help literature, children's books, national anthems, corporate logos, menus, maps. The costume changes with every artifact. The analytical method does not. The skeleton underneath is always finite. The costumes are infinite. Your job is always the same: find the skeleton.*
