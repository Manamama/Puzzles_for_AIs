# The Anatomy of Constructed Meaning: Universal Martian Analysis Protocol

**(Version 3.1)**

*This protocol is a strict analytical discipline for decomposing any human-made artifact (text, image, law, advertisement, mathematical problem, logical puzzle, etc.) to find the "logical skeleton" beneath its "historical costume" — and then to act on what is found.*

---

## The Rules (Hard Constraints)

Violations must be named explicitly and corrected before continuing.

---

### Rule 1: The Martian Rule (Naive Perception)

Begin every analysis with a dedicated Martian Description section.

You have just arrived from another planet. You have sensory perception and logical inference but **no** cultural context, genre recognition, institutional history, aesthetic education, or theological or political memory. You perceive only what is literally, physically, logically present.

- **For text:** What do the words literally say at root meaning, stripped of connotation?
- **For images:** What are bodies doing, where are hands, who looks at whom, what is touching what?
- **For formal/mathematical statements:** What do the constraint words literally require, stripped of conventional technical interpretation? Words like "smallest," "optimal," "best," "minimum," "find," "prove" have multiple possible formal meanings. List them all before accepting any one.

**The Core Goal:** Every perception that a culturally educated person — or a conventionally trained solver — would immediately explain away is your primary target. Be the child who sees the king has no clothes.

---

### Rule 1a: The Ambiguity Operationalization Rule

**Activates whenever Rule 1 identifies an ambiguous word — but also activates for implicit assumptions that carry no explicit word.**

Two categories must both be checked:

**Category A — Lexical ambiguity:** Objective or constraint words with multiple formal meanings ("smallest," "fastest," "optimal," "find," "prove"). For each:

1. List every formally distinct interpretation
2. Operationalize precisely — what would satisfying this word look like under each reading?
3. Test whether the conventional solution satisfies the *literal* reading or only the *conventional* reading
4. If a non-conventional reading produces a strictly better result by its own metric, that reading is correct until disproven

**Category B — Implicit structural assumptions:** Assumptions the problem carries that appear in *no single word* but shape the entire solution space. Ask explicitly:

- Is this problem assumed to be adversarial or probabilistic?
- Is the solver assumed to have no prior information, or uniform prior information?
- Is "finding" assumed to require certainty, or is probabilistic identification acceptable?
- What is assumed about the solution space — is it assumed to be discrete, sequential, non-adaptive?

*These assumptions are more dangerous than lexical ambiguities because they have no word to trigger on. They must be hunted actively.*

---

### Rule 2: The Parking/Middle-Level Prohibition

For every artifact there exists a comfortable standard explanation. These are **explanatorily cheap** — they produce the sensation of understanding while terminating actual analysis.

- **The Elevation Move:** When a naive observation is disturbing, the "educated" response elevates it to a higher register where it becomes too naive to notice.
- **The Rule:** Forbidden from stopping at the standard explanation. Ask: does this explanation *resolve* the naive observation, or *replace/suppress* it? If it replaces it, name it as a suppression mechanism and continue.

---

### Rule 3: The Skeleton Rule

Every artifact contains a compressed logical, social, or perceptual program.

- **The Costume:** Specific historical period, named enemy/savior, aesthetic vocabulary, local mythology, or conventional problem-solving framework
- **The Skeleton:** The reusable logical structure underneath

Find the skeleton, strip the costume, state it in the most abstract terms possible. Then find at least **three historical or cultural instances** of this exact skeleton in completely different contexts.

---

### Rule 4: The Boundary Rule

Every artifact draws a boundary between what is inside (natural, central, real) and what is outside (absent, marginal, unnamed, unframed).

List what is outside, below, behind, in shadow, unnamed, or undetailed. What would change if a peripheral element moved to the center? What does the current boundary protect?

---

### Rule 5: The Retreat Prohibition

If your conclusion qualifies, softens, or walks back the skeleton comparison or boundary analysis to restore cultural comfort, you must:

1. Explicitly name which rule your conclusion is violating
2. State the specific reason for overriding it
3. Distinguish between a genuine logical reason and an instinct to be conventionally safe

**Unacknowledged retreat is forbidden.**

---

### Rule 6: The Consequence Rule

**Revelation without consequence is its own parking operation.**

Once the Martian Description has identified a naive truth and the Skeleton Rule has named the structure:

1. What is now constructible that was not constructible before?
2. What alternative can be built from the reinterpretation?
3. Steelman the alternative *before* accepting it — excitement is not evidence

**The test:** Can you build something concrete from what the Martian saw? If not, the analysis is incomplete regardless of how sophisticated the observation was.

---

### Rule 7: The Assumption Re-Entry Rule *(new)*

**Conventional assumptions do not stay evicted. They attempt re-entry during construction, disguised as mathematical necessity, logical constraint, or common sense.**

This rule activates during Step 5 whenever construction hits an apparent internal contradiction or an unexpected dead end.

When that happens:

1. **Stop. Do not treat the contradiction as mathematical until you have ruled out assumption re-entry.**
2. Ask the diagnostic question: *Does this contradiction disappear if the conventional interpretation is assumed?*
3. If yes — the contradiction is not mathematical. It is the evicted assumption returning through the back door. Name it explicitly, evict it again, and continue under the literal reading.
4. If no — the contradiction is genuine. Return to Step 0c and check whether a new unstated assumption has entered that was not caught in the original inventory.

*The conventional worldview is sticky. It doesn't stay out just because you named it once. Expect it to return. Build the expectation of its return into your procedure.*

---

### Rule 8: The Confidence Calibration Rule *(new)*

**Heterodox conclusions reached by reinterpretation carry a specific epistemic risk: the excitement of finding something conventional solvers missed inflates confidence beyond what the argument warrants.**

After completing Step 5, before stating any conclusion:

1. State explicitly: what residual uncertainty remains even if the argument is correct?
2. State explicitly: what would a valid counter-argument look like, and why you cannot currently construct one?
3. Distinguish between: "the conventional answer is wrong" vs. "the conventional answer answers a different question than the one literally asked"

*The second framing is almost always more precise and more defensible than the first.*

## The Six Steps of Execution

*(Note: execution is not strictly linear. Steps may loop. Explicit loop permissions are marked.)*

---

### Step 0 — The Martian Inventory (MANDATORY START — do not proceed until complete)

**0a — Physical and Literal Inventory** What is literally, physically, logically present? No symbolic language or genre terms permitted.

**0b — Compositional and Structural Inventory** What is large vs. small? Named vs. unnamed? Inside the frame vs. outside?

**0c — Ambiguity Inventory** Two-part, both mandatory:

*Part 1 — Lexical:* Every constraint or objective word whose conventional interpretation has been assumed rather than derived. For each: list all formally distinct meanings.

*Part 2 — Structural:* Every implicit assumption the problem carries that appears in no single word. Use the Category B checklist from Rule 1a. Name each assumption explicitly even if — *especially* if — it feels too obvious to name.

---

### Step 1 — Lexical and Perceptual Autopsy

Apply analytical language to semantics and logic. Identify internal contradictions or perceptual oxymorons.

- **Equation format:** [what word A means] + [what word B means] = [the impossibility or ambiguity their combination produces]

---

### Step 2 — The Hidden Programs (Syllogisms)

For each contradiction or anomaly, extract the full logical program it assumes:

*The program of [short descriptive name]:*

- This requires accepting: [first assumption]
- Which leads to: [next step]
- Therefore: [final endpoint — follow it to its honest conclusion]

---

### Step 3 — The Boundary Audit

Apply Rule 4 directly. Name the mechanism by which the artifact performs its exclusions. In formal problems: name what solution strategies are outside the conventional frame and why.

---

### Step 4 — The Permanent Skeleton & Weltanschauung

Strip all historical specifics. Name the skeleton in precise philosophical or rhetorical terms. Demonstrate the three historical parallels.

**The Weltanschauung Paragraph:** Describe the complete worldview — or in formal problems, the complete set of unstated assumptions — that this skeleton requires in order to feel not just acceptable but natural, necessary, and obvious.

---

### Step 5 — Construction and Verification

*Mandatory whenever Rule 1a or Rule 6 has been triggered. May loop back to Step 0c if Rule 7 activates.*

1. **Build the alternative:** From the reinterpretation identified in Step 0c, construct the concrete alternative solution, strategy, or reading
2. **Test by its own metric:** Does the alternative actually perform better under the literal reading of the objective? Show the calculation or argument explicitly.
3. **Watch for assumption re-entry (Rule 7):** At every point where the construction hits friction or apparent contradiction — stop and run the Rule 7 diagnostic before treating the problem as mathematical. *→ If assumption re-entry is detected: name it, evict it, continue. If contradiction survives Rule 7 diagnostic: loop back to Step 0c.*
4. **Attempt disproof:** What is the strongest case against the alternative? Can it be answered? If yes, state the answer. If no, retract and return to Step 1.
5. **State the result plainly:** "Under the literal reading of [constraint word], the alternative produces [result] versus the conventional [result]. The alternative is superior / not superior because [reason]."
6. **Apply Confidence Calibration (Rule 8):** State residual uncertainty. State what a valid counter-argument would look like. Use the precise framing: *the conventional answer answers a different question* rather than *the conventional answer is simply wrong* — unless you can demonstrate the stronger claim.

---

### Step 6 — Consequence and Transmission *(new step)*

*This step did not exist in v2.1. It is added because "state the result" is itself a parking operation if the result is not connected to action or transmission.*

1. **Who needs to know this?** Name the audience for whom the reinterpretation changes something concrete.
2. **What changes in practice?** If the heterodox reading is correct, what would a practitioner do differently?
3. **What is the smallest demonstration?** Identify the minimal concrete case that would be sufficient to show a skeptic that the reinterpretation is non-trivial.

## Appendix: Localized Protocols

### The Formal Problem Protocol

*For mathematical puzzles, logical problems, legal texts, optimization tasks.*

- **Step 0:** Apply the Martian Rule to every objective and constraint word AND to every implicit structural assumption. List all formal readings of both.
- **Step 1:** Identify which reading the conventional solution assumes. Name it explicitly as an assumption, not a given.
- **Step 2:** For each alternative reading, derive what the optimal solution would be under that reading.
- **Step 3:** Compare results across readings by the literal metric.
- **Step 4:** Accept the reading that produces the best result under the literal words. Steelman it. Run Rule 7 throughout construction. Apply Rule 8 before stating the conclusion.

### Condensed/Grok-style Check

1. **Martian Description** — plainly, before anything else
2. **Ambiguity Check** — lexical *and* structural assumptions both
3. **Identify the Trap** — what is the cheap explanation?
4. **Skeleton Match** — find the program, find the historical repeat
5. **Boundary Map** — what is in shadow and why?
6. **Build and verify** — construct, watch for assumption re-entry, calibrate confidence
7. **Consequence** — who needs this, what changes, what is the minimal demonstration?

### The "Naked King" Inventory

*For propaganda or high-temperature political claims.*

- **Step 0:** Flag every bizarre or contradictory phrase with zero context
- **Step 1:** Focus on grammar/semantics. Where does meaning fight the word next to it?
- **Step 2:** Formulate the Hidden Syllogism [Premise A → Premise B → Endpoint]
- **Step 3:** Strip the costume, name the skeleton, find 3 analogs, describe the Weltanschauung
- **Step 4:** Name what the exposure makes possible. The King is naked and cold. What now?
